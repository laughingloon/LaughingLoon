There was always laugher when Connie was around. I loved Connie's wry sense of humor. Every time we talked, or saw each other we just made each other laugh.

She never lost her child like enthusiasm for the natural world. Her excitement, her eagerness to share this love, attracted like minds and hearts. Connie was the spark that ignited activities. Connie told me her greatest fear and her greatest love was for white water paddling. She took me under her wing and taught me to paddle. We hiked and skied. Cooked and attended concerts.

One of Connie's greatest gifts was to bring people together. We all just wanted to be with her. She was the axis her friends orbited around. The party didn't start until Connie arrived. She made a point of creating gatherings of friends. Connie loved to cook and food was love she shared eagerly with friends.

I always told her she was the best parent, a model, I held up to others, because of the person she raised single handedly, Alan, the most lively, kind, gentle man I know. She never tired of hearing that. How many other kids had a crazy, wild, whitewater woman for a mom? I never heard cross words between them.

A few weeks ago she was telling me about how much fun she had with her granddaughter at Caspian Lake. Just Tory and her playing on the beach, down at the boathouse, splashing and making up songs they sang together. I could imagine them playing, it made me smile.

She was kind. Her profession was health education. She cared deeply about the health of others. She had empathy for others.

Recently I only saw Connie in group gatherings. I feel so blessed to have spent a day, just the two of us, last July as we leisurely rolled our bikes downhill from the Green Mountains into the valley on a rail trail. We talked and stopped at every flower and plant Connie recognized. We listen to birds, stopped to admire views. After the bike trail we went for ice cream in St. Johnsbury. Connie insisted on showing me the amazing 15' x 15' Albert Bierstadt landscape painting "The Domes of Yosemite" in the St. Johnsbury Athenaeum, knowing as an artist I would appreciate it. Unfortunately the Athenaeum was closed, so instead she treated me to the Fairbanks Museum of natural history in St. J.

Connie was a positive force in this world. We will never know how many people she touched but we can be confident the number is vast.

Life changes in a moment. Sometimes we recognize this when it happens. Sometimes we look back and realize this person changed my life. Connie did this for me. She did this to everyone she met, though they may not have realized it. Now the moment has come where she is gone and we see how large a place in our hearts Connie occupied.

Each time I remember her passing I feel another arrow through my heart!

Peace my friend

Rob Macks
